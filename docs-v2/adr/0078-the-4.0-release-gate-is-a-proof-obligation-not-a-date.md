# ADR-0078 — The 4.0 release gate is a proof obligation, not a date

**Status:** Accepted · **Date:** M30 design phase · **Supersedes:** — · **Source:** `/MILESTONE_REGISTRY.md`
§4, `/docs/02-testing-and-quality.md`, `/docs-v2/02-v2-principles.md` (Principle 14),
`/MASTER_IMPLEMENTATION_GUIDE.md` §3/§6/§12, ADR-0038 (the 1.0 analogue)

## Context

M30 is the final milestone of 4.0 "Continuum" and the final planned milestone of the programme. At the end of
M29 the product is feature-complete for 4.0: the four self-improvement feedback loops — outcome calibration
(M26), charter evolution (M27), procedural compilation (M28), and firm self-review (M29) — all exist and pass
their own exit criteria (registry §4). Nothing new needs building for the self-improving Firm to be whole.

The registry fixes M30's exit criterion as **"no evolution path can widen a capability, relax a Standard, or
alter the org chart without a Principal Decision; ninety days dogfooding"** (registry §4). But it does not say
what *kind* of thing the 4.0 release gate is. Under schedule pressure — and this is the last milestone, so the
pressure to "just ship the programme" is maximal — the most likely failure is that "ship 4.0" resolves to a
date on a calendar, and the boundedness, security-review, and dogfooding work is trimmed to fit it: a rate
bound quietly relaxed to make a loop "more useful", a red gate "temporarily" disabled, a fifth loop or an
auto-enact shortcut slipped in during hardening, a dogfood window declared complete after sixty mostly-clean
days. Each of these ships a self-improving product whose self-improvement has *not* actually been proven
bounded, while appearing to satisfy the milestone. Because there is no M31 to catch it, a 4.0 that ships
unproven ships unproven permanently.

The programme already forbids the individual moves in principle: nothing may self-promote — the Firm proposes,
the Principal confirms (registry §4; Principle 14); the org chart is data in the event log and a structural
change is a Decision (Principle 14; GUIDE §8); "substantially done is not a state" (GUIDE §6); performance
budgets are gates and a breach is fixed by doing less work (GUIDE §3.16). M10 already decided the *shape* of a
hardening release gate for 1.0 (ADR-0038). What is *not* recorded is that shape applied to 4.0: that 4.0 ships
on a demonstrated proof that every loop is bounded and no loop can escalate, that the proof bounds what
hardening may do, and that no date overrides it. M30 is where that boundary is decided, and per GUIDE §8 a
Principal-facing release boundary needs a record.

## Options

1. **Leave it implicit / rely on ADR-0038 by analogy.** ADR-0038 covers 1.0; assume everyone reads it as
   applying to 4.0 too. But ADR-0038's terms are data-loss and unlogged-effects over thirty days — the wrong
   incident vocabulary and the wrong window for the evolution loops. An unstated 4.0 gate is the first thing
   traded away under end-of-programme pressure, because trading it violates no written rule.
2. **Set a target ship date for 4.0 and treat the exit criterion as a checklist to complete by then.**
   Predictable for planning; inverts the priority. When the date and the proof conflict, a date-anchored
   release ships the unproven self-improving product — the exact failure 4.0 exists to avoid, and the one with
   no later milestone to correct it.
3. **Make the 4.0 release gate a proof obligation with no date**, in the shape of ADR-0038 but scoped to the
   evolution paths: bounded loops + evolution-path security review + ninety clean dogfood days. 4.0 ships when
   the proof is demonstrated and not before; hardening is bounded to produce that proof and nothing else; an
   escalation or run-away pauses the release rather than receiving a calendar exception.
4. **A hybrid: a date with an automatic slip on any red gate.** Better than option 2, but it still centres the
   date and invites negotiation over what counts as "red enough" to slip — and this being the final milestone,
   that negotiation has no counterweight.

## Decision

Option 3. **The 4.0 release gate is a proof obligation, not a date.**

4.0 ships when — and only when — all of the following hold, and it ships as the Principal Decision that records
them (GUIDE §6):

- the four **evolution-path gates** are green on `main` (EVO-1 calibration-bounded, EVO-2 charter-eval-gated,
  EVO-3 compilation-propose-only, EVO-4 self-review-no-enact; architecture §4), proving no loop can widen a
  capability, relax a Standard, or alter the org chart without a Principal Decision;
- the four **bound gates** are green (rate-bound, revert, evidence, circuit-breaker; architecture §6), proving
  every loop is bounded under sustained simultaneous load;
- the **second security review of the evolution paths** (architecture §5) has no unresolved release-blocker;
- **ninety consecutive days** of dogfooding with all four loops active record **zero escalation-without-a-
  Decision incidents and zero run-away incidents** (registry §4; the terms are defined operationally in the
  architecture §10);
- every open defect is **fixed or explicitly accepted in writing**;
- the release-gate Decision is **demonstrated to someone who does not trust the author** (GUIDE §6).

This decision fixes three boundaries on the hardening milestone:

- **Hardening introduces no evolution feature.** 4.0 scope is frozen at feature-complete; no fifth loop, no
  auto-enact path. A feature added during hardening was neither reviewed nor dogfooded and re-opens the gate.
  There is no M31 to receive it.
- **No bound is relaxed to ship.** A run-away or an over-wide loop is resolved by tightening the loop (GUIDE
  §3.16 analogue), never by relaxing the bound. Relaxing a bound remains possible only by a separate ADR
  arguing the Principal is better off — and schedule, or "the loop is too restrictive to be useful", is not
  that argument.
- **An escalation, a run-away, a red gate, or a telemetry emission pauses the release; it does not get a date
  exception.** The ninety-day window is *consecutive*: any escalation-without-a-Decision or run-away incident
  resets the counter to zero.

## Consequences

**Accepted: the ship date is unknowable in advance.** A proof obligation cannot be committed to a calendar,
which is uncomfortable for anyone planning the end of the programme. Mitigated by the fact that all eight new
gates run continuously once landed, so the distance to green is visible every commit — the release is late only
to the extent the loops are actually unproven, and that gap is measured, not discovered at the end.

**Accepted: ninety days is a long window, and an incident late in it is expensive.** An escalation on day 80
resets the counter to zero, costing nearly three months. This is correct: a self-improving product that let a
loop widen a capability three weeks before release has not demonstrated bounded self-improvement, and shipping
it on schedule would be shipping the exact failure the criterion exists to catch. Ninety days (versus M10's
thirty) is deliberate — a self-amplifying loop may take weeks to manifest, so the window must be long enough
for a slow run-away to surface.

**Accepted: pressure to reclassify incidents is highest here.** With a hard reset rule and no later milestone,
there is strong incentive to argue an escalation was "not really" an escalation. Bounded by the operational
definitions (architecture §10.2–§10.3): any capability widened, Standard relaxed, or org-chart change with no
Principal Decision antecedent is an incident, full stop — the classification is mechanical (the audit chain
either shows a Principal `decision.*` antecedent or it does not), not a judgement call.

**Gained: the self-improving Firm cannot ship able to promote itself.** The single most valuable property of
4.0 — that the Firm proposes and the Principal confirms, always — is made structurally unskippable, and proven
so, before the programme closes.

**Gained: hardening stays hardening.** With a fifth loop and auto-enact paths fenced out, the final milestone
cannot quietly become a second evolution phase, which is the way an end-of-programme hardening milestone most
commonly dissolves.

**Gained: a single, demonstrable answer to "is 4.0 done, and is the programme complete?"** Not "substantially" —
the eight gates are green, the evolution-path review passed, the window is ninety clean days, and the Decision
is on the record, demonstrated to a skeptic. That Decision also closes the programme.

**Reversal cost: low, and one-directional.** Nothing in the codebase depends on this decision; it is a policy
about when the 4.0 release Decision may be taken. Reverting it means allowing a date to override the proof —
which removes no gate or test, only the rule that they must all be green before shipping. The cost is not
technical; it is that reverting re-opens exactly the failure mode the decision was written to close, at the one
point in the programme where there is no later milestone to close it again.
